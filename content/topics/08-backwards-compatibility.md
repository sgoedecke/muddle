---
title: Backwards compatibility
slug: backwards-compatibility
---

I would like to talk about backwards compatibility, because it is one of those topics that is easy to dismiss in the early stages of a project — when there are no users, when nothing is in production, when the cost of breaking a contract is zero — and that becomes existentially important very quickly, sometimes overnight, in a way that very few teams are prepared for. The transition from "we can change anything we want" to "we cannot change anything without considering the consequences for our users" is, in most projects, not a gradual one. It happens, suddenly, the first time you ship something to a real customer who builds something on top of it. After that moment, every API surface you ever exposed becomes a kind of permanent commitment, and the choices you made casually in the first six months become things you may be living with for the next ten years.

So I want to ask, gently: have you thought about which of your interfaces are public and which are private? Not in the sense of which ones have a `public` modifier on them in the language, but in the sense of which ones you are willing to commit to keeping stable for the lifetime of the system. This question is much harder to answer than people realise, because in modern systems, the boundary between "public" and "private" is fuzzy in ways that the documentation rarely acknowledges. Anything that is reachable will be reached. Any internal endpoint that exists for debugging will be discovered by a clever customer who needs it for something else. Any database column that is queryable will be queried by a downstream analytics pipeline that nobody told you about. Every behaviour your system has, intended or not, becomes a contract the moment any third party comes to depend on it. There is a well-known principle that captures this — that any observable behaviour of a system will be depended upon by somebody — and I have found it to be empirically true more often than I would have guessed.

The implication is that the surface area of your backwards-compatibility commitments is much larger than the surface area of your documented API. It includes the exact wording of your error messages, because someone is parsing them. It includes the exact ordering of fields in your JSON responses, because someone is relying on it. It includes the exact set of HTTP status codes you return for each failure mode, because someone has built retry logic that branches on them. It includes the timing of your responses, because someone has built timeouts that assume a certain latency profile. The only way to keep the surface area manageable is to be deliberate about it — to document, explicitly, what is a contract and what is an implementation detail, and to provide some mechanism (versioning, feature flags, deprecation periods) for evolving the contracts safely.

The standard mechanism for this is versioning, and versioning is one of those topics where almost every team has a slightly different opinion and almost no team is happy with the one they chose. URL versioning, header versioning, semantic versioning, calendar versioning, "we just promise not to break things" versioning — they all have trade-offs, and the trade-offs depend on the shape of your user base. If your users are other engineering teams within your own organisation, you can probably move fast and break things, because you can coordinate the breakage. If your users are external developers building businesses on top of your API, you cannot, because their businesses will fail when you break the API and they will, justifiably, hate you. If your users are end users using a web application, you are somewhere in between, because you control the client and can update it in lockstep, but only for users who refresh the page, which not all of them do as quickly as you might hope.

I want to flag, also, the depressing reality of deprecation. The standard playbook says: announce the deprecation, give users a grace period, monitor usage, and remove the deprecated feature when usage falls below some threshold. In practice, usage almost never falls below the threshold. The last percent of users are usually the ones who have invested the most in the deprecated feature, who have the least capacity to migrate, and who will be the angriest when you finally remove it. Many teams find themselves carrying deprecated features for years past their intended end-of-life, because the cost of removing them is higher than the cost of maintaining them, and they slowly accumulate into a kind of archaeological strata in the codebase that nobody fully understands. Avoiding this requires being honest, very early, about whether you are actually willing to enforce a deprecation, or whether you are just labelling things as deprecated as a kind of mild expression of regret.

There is also the question of data formats, which I think is the single most under-discussed aspect of backwards compatibility. A wire format that you ship today will be in some database, somewhere, in some customer's environment, for the next several decades. Your code will need to read it, even after you have moved on to a newer format, and the migration story has to actually work for every single record, not just the ones in your test suite. The systems that handle this well are systems that took it seriously from day one, that used self-describing formats with explicit version fields, that built migration tooling alongside every schema change. The systems that did not take it seriously end up with a kind of permanent fossil layer of historical records that nobody is sure how to interpret and that nobody is willing to delete.

I will stop here, but the short version is: every choice you make today is more permanent than you think, and the time to think about what you are committing to is now, while you still have the freedom to choose, rather than later, when the commitments have already locked themselves in.
