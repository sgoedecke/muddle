---
title: Error handling and observability
slug: error-handling
---

I want to spend some time on error handling and observability, which I am going to treat as a single topic because in practice they cannot be separated — the quality of your error handling determines what your observability tooling can see, and the quality of your observability tooling determines whether your error handling is doing what you think it is doing. They are two sides of the same coin, and getting either of them right in isolation is essentially impossible.

Let me start with error handling, because it is the more fundamental of the two. Every non-trivial piece of code can fail. Network calls can fail. Disk reads can fail. Memory allocations can fail. Parsing can fail. Even a simple addition can fail, in some languages, if it overflows. The question is not whether failures will occur but what your code does when they do, and this is a question that most codebases answer implicitly rather than explicitly, with results that are usually unfortunate.

The most common pattern I see is the swallowed exception: a try-catch block that catches an error, logs a message (or doesn't), and continues as if nothing had happened. This is almost always wrong, because it converts a failure into a silent corruption. The function that was supposed to write a record to the database returns normally, having failed to write the record, and now the rest of the system proceeds on the assumption that the record exists when in fact it does not. The bug that this produces will manifest, usually, weeks later, in a part of the system that has nothing to do with the original failure, and the person diagnosing it will have no chance of tracing it back to its root cause because the log message that would have explained it was discarded or never written. If you take only one thing from this letter, please take this: do not catch exceptions you do not know how to handle. Let them propagate. Crash the request, fail the job, return the error code. A loud failure is incomparably more debuggable than a silent corruption.

The corollary is that, at some level of your system, exceptions do need to be caught — at the boundary where a request enters or a job is dispatched — and at that level the catch needs to be principled. It needs to record what failed, with enough context to diagnose it. It needs to surface the failure to the user (or to the operator) in a form that is actionable. It needs to do whatever cleanup is required to leave the system in a consistent state. And it needs to do all of this without itself failing, because a failure handler that can fail is a failure handler that will, eventually, fail in a way that leaves you with no diagnostics at all.

That brings me to observability. The traditional answer to "how do I know what my system is doing" was logs, and logs are still useful, but they are not, by themselves, sufficient. A modern system produces enough log output that no human can read it in real time, and the discipline of writing good log messages — messages that are searchable, structured, and contain the right context — is harder than it looks and rarer than it should be. A log line that says "operation failed" is worse than no log line at all, because it convinces the operator that the log line is informative when in fact it tells them nothing. A log line that says "operation X failed for user Y in tenant Z with error E because of cause C in component D at timestamp T" is informative, but writing log lines like that consistently requires a discipline that almost no codebase maintains.

Metrics are the second pillar of observability, and they are, in some ways, more important than logs. Metrics tell you what is happening in aggregate: how often a thing is occurring, how its rate is changing, how it correlates with other things. The well-known failure mode here is to instrument the wrong things: to measure what is easy to measure rather than what matters. A common version of this is measuring averages — average latency, average error rate, average queue depth — when the interesting thing is almost always the tail. Your ninety-ninth percentile latency is, for most systems, a much better predictor of user experience than your mean latency, because users who hit the tail are the users who complain, and users who complain are the users who churn. Instrumentation that does not record tails is instrumentation that is hiding your worst problems from you, and you should be deeply suspicious of any dashboard that does not display percentiles.

Tracing is the third pillar, and it is the one most teams under-invest in. A distributed trace lets you follow a single request through every service it touches, with timing information at each hop. Without tracing, debugging cross-service issues is, frankly, guesswork: you correlate logs across services by timestamp, you piece together what happened from circumstantial evidence, and you accept that you will probably never know for sure what went wrong. With tracing, the answer is usually right in front of you within minutes. The catch is that tracing requires instrumentation at every service boundary, and instrumentation is one of those things that nobody wants to add until they urgently need it, by which point it is too late.

The fourth pillar, which I think deserves more attention than it usually gets, is the question of alerting. Observability data that nobody looks at is observability data that does not exist. Alerts need to fire when something is wrong, and not fire when nothing is wrong, and the gap between those two conditions is much smaller than people realise. Over-alerting is fatigue; under-alerting is silence in the face of crisis. Calibrating this is hard, ongoing work, and it requires somebody to own it, and most teams do not have that somebody.

So my request is: please, before you ship anything important, sit down and ask, "if this system fails in the middle of the night, how will I know, and how will I fix it?" If the answer is not specific and not rehearsed, the answer is "I will not."
