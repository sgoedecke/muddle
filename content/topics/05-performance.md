---
title: Performance under load
slug: performance
---

I want to talk for a while about performance, but specifically about performance under load, because in my experience these are two quite different topics and conflating them is the source of an enormous amount of grief. Performance in the small — the latency of a single request, the throughput of a single worker, the memory footprint of a single process — is interesting and worth measuring, but it is not the thing that takes systems down. What takes systems down is what happens when the small numbers interact: when many requests arrive at once, when many workers compete for the same lock, when many processes try to allocate memory from the same constrained pool. Those interactions almost never look like the sum of their parts. They look, instead, like emergent failure modes that nobody saw coming because nobody could have seen them by staring at the small numbers in isolation.

Let me give you some specific worries.

The first is queueing. Almost every system, at every layer, contains queues — explicit ones like message buses and thread pools, and implicit ones like TCP socket buffers and CPU run queues. Under steady-state load, queues are invisible. Under bursty load, they fill up. And once they fill up, they do not gracefully degrade; they collapse, in a manner that is mathematically predictable and emotionally devastating. The latency you observe is no longer the latency of doing the work; it is the latency of doing the work plus waiting in line behind everyone else who is also waiting in line. I have seen systems that ran at one hundred milliseconds per request at fifty requests per second become five-second-per-request systems at sixty requests per second, with no warning and no intermediate state. If you have not measured your queueing behaviour under load — and I mean really measured it, with realistic traffic patterns, not synthetic constant-rate benchmarks — then you do not actually know how your system behaves. You know how your system behaves on a good day.

The second is the question of timeouts. Every network call has a timeout, whether you set one or not; the default is usually "wait forever," which is almost never what you want. The interesting thing about timeouts is that they cascade. If service A calls service B with a thirty-second timeout, and service B calls service C with a thirty-second timeout, and service C is slow, then service B will hold a connection to service A open for the full thirty seconds while waiting on C, and during those thirty seconds it cannot serve any other request that arrives on the same worker. This is how a small upstream problem becomes a large downstream outage. The fix is to set conservative timeouts everywhere and to make them shorter as you go up the call stack, but very few systems do this carefully, and you can see the consequences in any major incident retrospective from the past decade.

The third worry, and one that I think gets dramatically less attention than it deserves, is the question of retries. Retries are the single most dangerous tool in the distributed systems toolbox. Used well, they paper over transient failures and improve user experience. Used badly, they take a struggling system and finish it off. When a service starts to fail, every client that retries puts additional load on the failing service, which makes it fail harder, which causes more retries, which causes more load. This is called a retry storm, and it is the proximate cause of a depressing number of catastrophic outages. The mitigation is well known — exponential backoff with jitter, circuit breakers, retry budgets — but it has to be implemented at every layer of the system, and one client that retries naively is enough to undo all the careful work done elsewhere. I would urge you to audit, end to end, exactly which calls in your system retry, on what schedule, with what cap, and whether the answer changes when the system is unhealthy.

The fourth worry is the question of cold caches. Most systems perform well when their caches are warm and badly when they are cold. A cache miss can be a hundred times slower than a cache hit, and a system that is provisioned for the hit rate it sees during steady-state operation can be wildly underprovisioned for the moment its caches are wiped — by a deployment, by a node failure, by a configuration change, by a power event. The classic and embarrassing version of this is the system that, having just survived a brief outage, recovers, only to immediately fall over again as every client hits it with the requests it queued up during the downtime, against caches that have not yet rewarmed. Recovering from an outage is, in some ways, more dangerous than the outage itself, because the failure mode is rarer and therefore less practised.

The fifth worry, and I will stop after this one, is the question of head-of-line blocking. Many systems serve requests in the order they arrive on a connection, and many systems serialise work behind shared resources — a database connection, a write lock, a single-threaded event loop. When one request is slow, every request behind it is slow, even if the other requests would have been fast on their own. This is one of the reasons that a small number of slow requests can ruin the experience of a much larger number of users. The remediation is usually some combination of timeouts, isolation, and concurrency, but the right answer depends very specifically on the shape of your system, and I do not think it can be determined in the abstract.

What I am really asking, underneath all of this, is whether you have a model — even an informal, hand-drawn, back-of-the-envelope model — of how your system behaves at the limits. Most teams do not. They have a model of how their system behaves in the middle, and they hope that the limits are far enough away that they will never have to think about them. The limits are, in my experience, never as far away as people think.
