---
title: Onboarding for new contributors
slug: onboarding
---

I want to spend some time on the question of onboarding new contributors, because it is one of those topics that almost every project under-invests in until the moment when under-investment becomes impossible to ignore, which is usually the moment when the original authors of the system have all left and nobody who remains can confidently explain how anything works. The cost of poor onboarding is not paid by the people who designed the system, who have it all in their heads anyway. It is paid by everyone who comes after them, and it is paid in proportion to how many of those people there will eventually be. For a system that you expect to be maintained for any meaningful period, the cumulative cost of poor onboarding can easily exceed the cost of the original development.

The first question I would ask is: how long does it take, today, for a new contributor to make their first meaningful change to the codebase? If you have not measured this, I would strongly recommend that you do. The number is usually much larger than the project's owners think. A "first meaningful change" should not mean "a typo fix"; it should mean something like "a substantive change to a non-trivial piece of behaviour, with appropriate tests, that gets merged." In well-onboarded projects, this happens within the first week. In poorly-onboarded projects, it can take months. The difference is not a matter of the contributors' talent; it is a matter of the project's infrastructure for bringing them up to speed.

The second question is: what is the path a new contributor walks, from the moment they decide they want to contribute, to the moment they have a development environment running and a passing test suite? If that path has more than five or six steps, and if any of those steps depend on tacit knowledge that is not documented anywhere, you are filtering out contributors who would otherwise have helped you. Many of them will not push through the friction. They will fork the repo, struggle for an afternoon, give up, and never tell you why. You will see this only in the aggregate, as a slow flow of would-be contributors that mysteriously never converts to actual contributions, and you will, in most cases, blame the contributors rather than the friction.

The third question is: what does the conceptual onramp look like? A new contributor needs to know not just how to run the system, but how to think about the system — what its major components are, how they fit together, what design constraints shaped the architecture, what alternatives were considered and rejected and why. This is the kind of context that lives, by default, only in the heads of the original engineers, and that is invisible to everyone else. Capturing it in writing takes real effort, but the alternative is to require every new contributor to derive it for themselves through painful trial and error, which wastes everyone's time. A serious onboarding investment includes architecture documents, design history, and a curated list of "good first issues" that introduce the contributor to different parts of the system progressively.

The fourth question, which is harder, is about the social dimension of onboarding. A new contributor who opens their first pull request is in a vulnerable position. They are exposing their work to an established group of strangers, asking for feedback, hoping not to look stupid. How that first interaction goes will, for most contributors, determine whether they ever contribute again. Reviewers who respond promptly, who engage constructively, who explain their feedback in terms the contributor can learn from, who praise what is good as well as critiquing what is not — these reviewers retain contributors. Reviewers who are slow, who are terse, who are condescending, who treat the review as an opportunity to demonstrate their own superiority — these reviewers drive contributors away, and the contributors do not come back, and they tell their friends not to come either. The reputation of a project for being welcoming or hostile spreads much faster than anyone realises.

The fifth question is about the long arc of contributor development. Onboarding is not just the first week; it is the months and years over which a contributor goes from "I can fix small bugs" to "I can design and ship significant features" to "I can mentor other contributors." Each of these transitions requires deliberate support: the contributor needs to be given progressively harder problems, needs to be trusted with progressively more responsibility, needs to have their growth recognised in some way that signals to them that they are valued. Projects that do this well end up with a deep bench of contributors who can step into ownership roles as the original authors move on. Projects that do not end up with a permanent bottleneck around the original authors, who become irreplaceable in a way that is bad for them, bad for the project, and bad for everyone who depends on it.

The sixth question, which I will end on, is about turnover. Contributors leave. They get other jobs, they burn out, they change interests, they have children. This will happen to your contributors too. The question is whether the project survives when they do, and the answer depends entirely on whether their knowledge was captured and whether their work was distributed enough that someone else can pick it up. The bus factor of your project is a real thing, and it tends to drift toward one over time unless you actively work against it. The countermeasure is not to require every contributor to be a generalist, but to ensure that every part of the system has at least two people who could maintain it, and to act, deliberately, to grow the second person whenever you find a part of the system with only one.

I will stop here. The short version is: every hour you invest in making new contributors successful pays back many times over, and most projects invest none.
