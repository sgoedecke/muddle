---
title: Safety
slug: safety
---

I have been thinking a great deal lately about what we actually mean when we say a system is "safe," and I have come to believe that most projects, in their early stages, do not have a clear enough answer to that question. Safety is one of those words that everyone agrees is important and almost nobody can define crisply when pressed. So I want to use this space to raise some of the questions that I think you and your collaborators ought to be asking yourselves, ideally before — but realistically during — the work that you are doing now.

The first question, and in some ways the most important, is: safe for whom? It is very easy to design a system that is safe for the people who built it and for the people who look most like the people who built it, and to discover, only much later and at considerable embarrassment, that it is not at all safe for users with slightly different needs, slightly different contexts, slightly different threat models. I do not want to lecture you on this, because I am sure you have read the same essays I have. But I do want to ask, in a concrete and specific way: have you written down, anywhere, the full list of populations who might use or be affected by this system? And for each of them, have you considered what "safety" means from their perspective? Because if you have not, you will almost certainly end up, at some point, with a polite but devastating message in your inbox from someone explaining the harm your design has done to them, and at that point you will be doing remediation under emotional and political pressure, which is much harder than doing it now.

The second question — and this one is genuinely difficult, so I do not blame anyone for not having a complete answer — is: what are the failure modes, and how bad are they? It is one thing to ship a system that, in the worst case, returns the wrong number on a dashboard. It is another to ship a system that, in the worst case, sends a notification to the wrong person, or charges the wrong credit card, or deletes a user's data without warning. I am not claiming your project is in the second category. I am only saying that, before you ship, somebody should have sat down and written out the worst-case scenarios in plain language, ranked them by severity, and made a deliberate decision about which ones are acceptable and which ones require additional engineering before launch. If that document does not exist, you do not yet know what your safety profile is; you only know what you hope it is.

I want to also press, gently, on the question of reversibility. The systems I have seen go most catastrophically wrong are the ones that took irreversible actions on the basis of fallible inputs. If your system performs any action that cannot be undone — sending an email, making a payment, modifying an external record, deleting a row — then every single piece of code that leads to that action should be treated with a level of paranoia that is, frankly, exhausting to maintain. There should be confirmations. There should be dry-run modes. There should be audit logs that survive even when the primary database does not. There should be rate limits not just on the user-facing API but on the irreversible internal action itself, so that even a runaway internal bug cannot, say, mail-bomb your entire user base over the course of a single bad afternoon. I have seen the alternative, and the alternative is not a good time for anyone.

There is also a quieter, sadder kind of safety question, which is the question of who you trust and how much. Every system has trust boundaries: the user trusts the client, the client trusts the server, the server trusts its database, the database trusts the underlying disk. At each of those boundaries, you have made an implicit assumption: that the entity on the other side of the boundary is, in some sense, well-behaved. Those assumptions are almost always wrong eventually. The user pastes a malformed payload. The client gets compromised by a browser extension. The server is briefly run with the wrong configuration during an incident. The database is restored from a backup that turns out to be a week stale. The disk silently corrupts a sector. Safety, at its core, is the discipline of asking, at every trust boundary, "what does my code do when the thing on the other side lies to me?" And of being honest about the answers.

I would be remiss not to mention, finally, the social dimension of safety, which is harder to talk about and easier to ignore. A system can be technically secure, technically reliable, technically correct, and still cause real harm to real people because of the way it interacts with social dynamics that the designers did not anticipate. Features that seem neutral — public profiles, default-on notifications, automatic friend suggestions, behavioural nudges — can become vectors for stalking, harassment, and coercion. I am not suggesting your project has any of these features. I am only suggesting that, before you ship anything that touches other humans even indirectly, somebody should sit down and play out, in their head, the most malicious possible use of each feature, and ask whether the design makes that use easier or harder. If the answer is "easier," you have a problem you can address now for the cost of an afternoon, or address later for the cost of someone's safety.

I will leave it there for now. I hope none of this came across as alarmist. I do not think your project is uniquely dangerous; I think every project of this shape is dangerous in ways that are easy to underestimate, and I would much rather raise the worries now, while they are cheap to address, than wait until they are expensive.
