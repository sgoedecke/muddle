---
title: Mobile responsiveness
slug: mobile
---

I want to talk for a while about mobile responsiveness, because it is a topic that almost every team claims to take seriously and that almost no team actually does. The standard story is that the site or application is "responsive" — that it has been built with mobile in mind, that it has been tested on a phone, that breakpoints exist in the CSS. This story is almost never the whole truth. What is usually true is that someone, at some point, opened the site on their personal phone, observed that it did not look obviously broken, and pronounced the mobile experience acceptable. This is not engineering. This is hope.

So let me lay out, as best I can, what I think serious mobile responsiveness actually requires.

The first thing is to recognise that "mobile" is not a single device class but a vast and growing spectrum. The mental model of "a phone" that most engineers carry around is, usually, a recent high-end iPhone or a flagship Android device, with a fast processor, a large screen, a recent operating system, and a fast network connection. This is not the median mobile device in the world. The median mobile device in the world is an older mid-range Android phone, possibly several years out of date, with a slower processor, a smaller screen, an older operating system, and a slower and more expensive network connection. A site that works beautifully on the engineer's iPhone may be unusable on the median device, and the engineer will not notice because the engineer does not own the median device. The single most useful thing you can do to improve your mobile responsiveness is to acquire and regularly use a representative low-end Android device. Until you have felt how slow your own product is in that environment, you cannot really know what your users are experiencing.

The second thing is to recognise that mobile network conditions are dramatically more variable than desktop network conditions. A user on a phone may be on fast wifi, slow wifi, fast cellular, slow cellular, or no connectivity at all, and these conditions can change from one second to the next as the user moves around the world. A site that works well only under good network conditions is a site that works badly for a large fraction of mobile sessions. The mitigations are well known — lazy loading, progressive enhancement, aggressive caching, offline support, careful handling of network failures — but they require deliberate engineering, and almost no codebase implements them by default.

The third thing is to recognise that touch interfaces are not just mouse interfaces with bigger click targets. Touch interactions have fundamentally different ergonomics. There is no hover state. There is no precise pointer. There is no concept of "right-click." There is a viewport that is partially obscured by a virtual keyboard much of the time. There are gestures — swipes, pinches, long presses — that compete with the browser's own gestures and with the operating system's gestures, in ways that depend on context. Designing well for touch requires actually using a touch device and noticing what feels right and what feels wrong, and it requires being willing to make different design choices than you would make for a mouse, rather than treating touch as a degraded form of mouse.

The fourth thing is to recognise that mobile screens are not just small desktop screens. The information density that works on a wide desktop monitor does not work on a phone, because there simply is not the physical space for it. A serious mobile design is not "the desktop design with the columns rearranged"; it is a reconsidered presentation of the underlying content, in which choices have been made about what to show, what to hide, what to summarise, and what to defer to a second screen. This requires product judgement, not just CSS. Teams that try to solve mobile responsiveness purely in the layout layer end up with mobile experiences that are technically functional but practically miserable, because every screen has too much on it and the user has to scroll for half a minute to find the action they came for.

The fifth thing is the question of the virtual keyboard, which is one of the most consistently mishandled aspects of mobile design. When the keyboard appears, it covers a significant fraction of the screen. The element the user is typing into may or may not remain visible. The submit button may or may not be reachable. Custom inputs that look like text fields but are not actually text fields may invoke the wrong keyboard, or no keyboard at all, leading to bizarre and unaccountable user behaviour. Even basic things — using the right `inputmode`, the right `autocomplete` attributes, the right `enterkeyhint` — are routinely neglected, and the cumulative effect is a mobile form-filling experience that feels, to the user, like wading through tar.

The sixth thing is the question of performance, which on mobile is dramatically more punishing than on desktop. A JavaScript bundle that loads instantly on a desktop with a fast network and a fast CPU can take ten or fifteen seconds to parse and execute on a budget Android device on a slow connection. A page that animates smoothly on a desktop can stutter visibly on a phone, especially under battery saver mode. Memory pressure is much higher; the browser is more likely to evict your page from memory if the user switches apps, with the result that returning to the page requires a full reload. The discipline of treating performance as a feature, not an afterthought, is much more important on mobile than on desktop, and the consequences of neglecting it are much more visible.

I will stop here, but the short version is: mobile is not a smaller desktop, and treating it as one will produce a mobile experience your users politely tolerate rather than enjoy. The teams that take it seriously, that test on real devices, that design for touch from the beginning, that performance-budget aggressively, end up with mobile experiences that users prefer to the desktop equivalent. That is the bar I would urge you to aim for.
