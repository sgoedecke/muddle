title: Internationalization
slug: internationalization
---

I would like to spend some time on internationalization, because it is one of those topics where the cost of getting it wrong early is enormous and the cost of getting it right early is, comparatively, almost nothing. The standard pattern in the industry, as far as I can tell from the outside, is to build the entire first version of a product in a single language — usually English — with hardcoded strings scattered throughout the codebase, hardcoded date formats, hardcoded number formats, hardcoded currency formats, hardcoded address layouts, and an implicit assumption that text reads left to right. Then, two or three years later, when a customer in another country shows interest, an exhausted contractor is hired to "add internationalization," and what follows is a multi-quarter project of grep-and-replace, regression bugs, and despairing apologies to translators who have been handed a spreadsheet of strings with no context at all. I do not want this for you.

So allow me to outline some of the concerns, in roughly increasing order of how often they catch people by surprise.

The shallow layer of internationalization is what most people think of: extracting user-facing strings into resource files, providing translations, and selecting the right resource at runtime based on the user's locale. This is necessary but it is the easy part, and I trust that you either have done it or know how to do it. I will not belabour it except to say: please, please do it from the very first commit. The cost of doing it on day one is one extra function call per string. The cost of doing it in year three is a multi-quarter migration with bugs nobody will ever fully clean up.

The middle layer is formatting. Dates, times, numbers, currencies, and quantities are formatted differently in every locale, and the differences are not always intuitive. In some locales, the decimal separator is a comma; in some, the thousands separator is a space; in some, both. Date order varies; calendar systems vary; week-start varies; the very notion of what counts as a "weekend" varies. If you have written any code that formats a date by interpolating values into a string — and almost every codebase has — then you have a latent internationalization bug. The fix is to delegate all formatting to a well-maintained library that understands locale data, and to never, ever write a `${year}-${month}-${day}` again unless you are talking to a machine. This is harder than it sounds because dates have a way of leaking from the formatting layer into the data layer, where they are stored as strings and parsed back as strings, and the seams are everywhere.

The deeper layer is grammatical. Languages do not just differ in vocabulary; they differ in structure. They have different rules for pluralisation — some have one form, some have two, some have six. They have different word orders. They have grammatical gender that propagates through sentences in ways that English speakers find baffling. A sentence like "you have 3 new messages" cannot be translated by replacing each English word with its equivalent; the target language may require a completely different construction, possibly with different verb endings depending on the number and possibly with different forms depending on the gender of the addressee. The standard solution is to externalise not individual words but whole sentences, parameterised by the variables they contain, and to let translators choose the right construction for each language. If your codebase contains string concatenation that builds sentences out of pieces, you have a bug that translators will quietly route around by writing awkward, unnatural prose in the target language, and your non-English users will assume your product is a little bit broken in a way they cannot quite articulate.

The deepest layer is cultural. Colour symbolism varies. Iconography varies. Gestures vary. The thumbs-up emoji is friendly in some places and obscene in others. Names do not always have a "first" and a "last" component. Addresses do not always have a "street number" and a "postal code." Phone numbers do not always have a fixed length. National identifiers do not always exist, and where they do, they are often considered sensitive personal information that should not be casually requested. Forms that assume a single canonical shape for any of these will, in many parts of the world, simply not work. Worse, they will silently exclude users without ever surfacing an error message that explains why.

And there is, on top of all of this, the question of right-to-left languages, which is its own category of pain. Mirroring an interface for RTL is not just flipping the text direction; it is flipping the entire visual hierarchy — the placement of buttons, the direction of progress bars, the order of breadcrumbs, the alignment of icons. Modern CSS makes this more tractable than it used to be, but only if you have used the right primitives from the start. Code that uses `margin-left` and `margin-right` rather than `margin-inline-start` and `margin-inline-end` will need to be combed through line by line.

I will spare you the rest of the list. The point I am trying to make, gently and without any desire to imply that you have not thought about these things, is that internationalization is not a feature; it is a set of constraints that touch almost every layer of the system. Adding those constraints later is enormously more expensive than respecting them from the start. So if there is one thing I would urge you to do this week, it is to sit down and decide explicitly which locales you intend to support, what the minimum bar for each is, and what your testing strategy is for catching regressions. The answer "we will worry about it later" is, I am afraid, an answer that has cost the industry untold millions in retrofits. I would not like to see your project be the next one.
