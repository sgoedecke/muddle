---
title: Accessibility
slug: accessibility
---

I want to talk for a while about accessibility, because I have noticed, over years of looking at projects very much like yours, that this is one of those concerns that everyone agrees is important and almost everyone treats as a backlog item to be addressed "in a future release." That phrase — "in a future release" — is, in my experience, one of the most reliable warning signs in software. It almost always means: never, or at least not until a paying customer complains loudly enough that we are forced to do it badly, under time pressure, with no budget and no expertise.

So please permit me a few thoughts, in no particular order, on what I think a serious approach to accessibility looks like, and on the specific failure modes that I have seen recur in projects that did not take it seriously enough early on.

The first thing I want to say is that accessibility is not a single feature. It is not a checklist you can complete and then move on from. It is, much more like security or reliability, a property of the entire system that emerges (or fails to emerge) from thousands of small decisions made at every layer of the stack. The button that lacks a discernible label, the colour contrast that hovers just under the threshold, the form that submits via a keyboard shortcut that conflicts with a screen reader's command, the modal that traps focus in the wrong place — none of these are big bugs individually, but in aggregate they are the difference between a product that some of your users can use and a product that all of your users can use. And the depressing thing is that, once you have shipped, the cost of going back and fixing each of those small decisions is enormous, because each of them is entangled with every other decision built on top of it.

So if I may, I would urge you to think about accessibility not as a feature to be added but as a constraint to be respected. Every component you write should, from the first commit, be keyboard navigable. Every colour you choose should, from the first design pass, meet contrast requirements. Every image you embed should, from the first content draft, have meaningful alternative text — and I mean meaningful, not the dreaded "image" or "photo" or, worst of all, "alt text here." Every form you build should be testable by someone with a screen reader, and ideally tested by someone with a screen reader, not just by someone running an automated audit tool. The automated tools are useful — they catch the most embarrassing failures — but they catch perhaps a third of the real problems, and they are completely blind to the most important question, which is whether the experience is actually pleasant to use with assistive technology.

I want to also raise the question of cognitive accessibility, which I have noticed gets very little airtime even in serious accessibility discussions. Cognitive accessibility means designing for users whose attention, working memory, language comprehension, or executive function differ from the median in any direction. This is a much larger population than people realise, and it includes not only users with diagnosed cognitive differences but also users who are tired, distracted, stressed, intoxicated, multilingual, or simply having a bad day. A system that is cognitively accessible is one that has short, plain sentences in its UI; that puts the most important information first; that does not require users to hold complex state in their heads; that gives unambiguous feedback after every action; that lets users undo, redo, and recover from mistakes without penalty. Most software is bad at this, and being bad at it is so normalised that almost nobody notices.

There is also the question of motor accessibility. I would gently encourage you to consider: can your interface be operated entirely without a mouse? Can it be operated by someone using only a single switch input, perhaps via a sip-and-puff device? Can it be used by someone with a tremor, who may inadvertently double-click or who may struggle to hit small targets? The answers to these questions almost always reveal small design choices that exclude users for no good reason — a click target that is twelve pixels tall, a drag-and-drop interaction with no keyboard equivalent, a hover-triggered tooltip whose contents are only available to users with a steady hand and a working pointer.

I want to mention, before I run out of breath, the question of accessibility for assistive technology that does not yet exist. Standards evolve. New devices appear. Voice-driven interfaces, gaze-tracking interfaces, brain-computer interfaces — some of these are still science fiction, but some of them are arriving faster than anyone expected. The best preparation for unknown future assistive technology is to follow semantic markup conventions strictly: use the correct HTML elements for the correct purposes, expose state via ARIA attributes when necessary and not when unnecessary, and avoid the temptation to reinvent native controls in custom JavaScript. Every time you build a custom dropdown or a custom checkbox or a custom slider, you are committing to maintaining accessibility for that component forever, against an evolving landscape of assistive technologies, and you will almost certainly fail at this, because the platform vendors have already done that work for you in the native equivalents.

The last thing I want to say is something a little more personal, and I hope you will not take it the wrong way. I have known several engineers who, over the course of their career, lost some portion of their own vision or hearing or motor control, sometimes suddenly, and who found themselves unable to use the very systems they had spent their working lives building. It is a very particular kind of regret. I would not wish it on anyone. So please: build the kind of software that you, in twenty or thirty years, would still be able to use. That is, I think, a useful test.
