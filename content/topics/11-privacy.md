---
title: Privacy
slug: privacy
---

I want to talk about privacy, and I want to do so in a way that goes beyond the legal framing — beyond GDPR and CCPA and the alphabet soup of compliance regimes — because I think the legal framing, while important, is the floor of what privacy means rather than the ceiling. A system can be perfectly compliant with every applicable regulation and still treat its users' data in ways that, if those users fully understood, they would find deeply objectionable. The opposite is also true: a system can occasionally fall short of some technical requirement of some particular regulation and still, in spirit and in practice, treat its users' data with care and respect. I am much more interested in the latter than in the former, and I would urge you to be as well.

The first and most important question about privacy is: what data do you collect, and why? I have seen, over the years, that the default in most engineering organisations is to collect everything that can be collected, on the assumption that it might be useful someday. Pageviews, click events, scroll depths, form interactions, device fingerprints, IP addresses, referrers, session recordings — all of it gathered, all of it stored, all of it lurking in some warehouse waiting for a future product manager to find a use for it. This default is, I think, ethically lazy and operationally dangerous. It is ethically lazy because it treats the user's data as something to be hoarded rather than as something held in trust. It is operationally dangerous because every byte of data you store is a byte of data you have to protect, a byte of data that can be subpoenaed, a byte of data that can leak. The systems with the strongest privacy postures are not the systems with the best compliance programmes; they are the systems that simply do not have very much data in the first place.

So please, before you instrument anything new, ask yourself the question: what specific decision is this data going to inform? If you cannot answer that question crisply, do not collect the data. Wait until you know what you want to know, and then collect the minimum data required to answer that specific question. This is the inverse of the standard advice — "instrument everything, ask questions later" — and I am aware that it sounds quaint. But the standard advice has produced an industry-wide privacy disaster, and the inverse has produced systems that are dramatically easier to reason about, dramatically cheaper to operate, and dramatically less embarrassing when, eventually, somebody asks what you actually have on file.

The second question is: who has access to the data, internally? It is depressingly common for organisations to have controls in place against external attackers but to have essentially no controls against insider misuse. Every engineer in the company can run arbitrary SQL queries against the production database. Every analyst can pull any user's full history into a spreadsheet. Every customer support representative can view the contents of any account, without notification and without audit. This is a privacy posture by default, and it is almost never the privacy posture anyone would have chosen if they had thought about it explicitly. The mitigation is not glamorous: it is role-based access controls, audit logging, query logging, periodic access reviews, and the willingness to actually revoke access from people who do not need it. None of this is fun. All of it is necessary.

The third question is: what is your data retention policy, and is it actually enforced? The answer in most organisations is "we keep everything forever, because storage is cheap and nobody has gotten around to writing a deletion job." This is, again, a position rather than a choice, and it is a position that becomes a problem the moment anyone asks for their data to be deleted, or the moment a court order requires the production of records you did not realise you still had. A serious retention policy specifies, for each category of data, how long it is kept, when it is deleted, and how the deletion is verified. Writing such a policy is tedious. Implementing it is more tedious. Operating it in perpetuity is yet more tedious. The alternative, when something eventually goes wrong, is dramatically less tedious and much more public.

The fourth question is: what happens to the data when it leaves your system? Modern systems do not live in isolation. They ship logs to a third-party aggregator, send metrics to a monitoring vendor, push events to an analytics platform, sync records to a CRM, feed numbers into a dashboard tool that someone in the finance department uses. Each of those destinations is a place your users' data is now living, and each of them is governed by a different set of policies, contracts, and security postures. The question "what happens to a user's email address when they sign up?" should have a complete, traceable answer that names every system that address ends up in, and the people answering the question should not have to guess. In most organisations, they do.

The fifth question, which I will end on, is about consent. Real consent is informed, specific, and revocable. Most of what passes for consent on the modern web is none of these things: it is a cookie banner clicked through without reading, a checkbox pre-ticked, a "by signing up you agree to" footer at the bottom of a page nobody scrolled to. Building a system that obtains real consent is harder than building a system that collects the legally-minimal version of consent, but it is also dramatically more trustworthy, and trust, in the end, is what privacy is really about. People are starting to notice which products treat them as adults and which do not, and the ones that do are starting to win.

I will stop. The shortest version of the whole thing is: collect less, protect more, delete on schedule, and be honest about what you are doing.
