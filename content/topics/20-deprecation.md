title: Long-term deprecation and migration
slug: deprecation
---

I would like to close — well, I say "close," but I have learned over the years that one's intentions about closing rarely survive contact with the actual writing — with the question of long-term deprecation and migration paths. This is, in some sense, the bookend to my earlier note about backwards compatibility, and I think it deserves its own treatment, because the practice of deprecating something well is a craft that very few projects ever really master.

The basic problem is this: any non-trivial system will, over its lifetime, accumulate features, interfaces, and conventions that, in hindsight, turn out to have been mistakes. Sometimes the mistake is technical — the feature does not perform well at scale, or it interacts badly with other features, or it has security implications that were not appreciated at the time. Sometimes the mistake is product — the feature did not deliver the value its designers expected, or it serves a use case that has since become obsolete, or it has been superseded by a better alternative. In either case, the project is left with a piece of itself that it wishes it did not have, and the question becomes: how do you get rid of it?

The naive answer is: just delete it. This works only when nobody is using the thing in question, and "nobody is using the thing in question" is almost never as true as you hope it is. Real systems have users, and users build on whatever is available, and they do not always tell you what they have built. Any feature that has been in production for more than a few months will, almost certainly, have at least some users depending on it, and removing it without warning will break their workflows in ways that range from inconvenient to catastrophic.

So the considered answer involves a deprecation process, and a deprecation process has, in my experience, several distinct phases, each of which projects routinely conflate or skip.

The first phase is the announcement: telling users that the feature will, at some future date, be removed. The announcement needs to be in a place users will see it — release notes, in-product banners, email, blog posts, whatever channels you actually use to reach the user base — and it needs to be specific. "We may remove this feature in the future" is not a deprecation announcement; it is a vague gesture toward one. "We will remove this feature on the first of January next year, here is what you can use instead, here is documentation on how to migrate, here is the email address to write to if you need help" is a deprecation announcement.

The second phase is the migration period: the window during which both the deprecated feature and its replacement coexist, and users can transition at their own pace. This window needs to be long enough that users with realistic constraints can actually complete the migration, which usually means at least one full release cycle of every system that depends on the deprecated feature. For systems with enterprise customers on long planning cycles, this can mean a year or more. Compressing this window is the single most common mistake I see, and it is a mistake that converts users from grateful collaborators into angry refugees.

The third phase is the measurement: actually tracking who is still using the deprecated feature, and how, and on what trajectory. This sounds obvious, but it is depressingly often skipped. Many deprecations proceed entirely on the basis of assumption — "we assume usage is low by now" — without any actual measurement of whether that is true. The result is that the removal date arrives, the feature is removed, and a long-tail of users who never got the message suddenly find themselves with broken systems and no path forward. Instrument the deprecated feature. Watch the usage curve. Reach out, individually if necessary, to the largest remaining users. Adjust the timeline if the data demands it.

The fourth phase is the actual removal, which should, ideally, be anticlimactic. By the time you remove the feature, usage should be effectively zero, the documentation should be updated, the migration guide should be battle-tested, and the support team should be ready for the small number of laggards who will inevitably show up only after the removal. If any of these is not true, you are not ready to remove the feature; you are ready to cause an incident.

The fifth phase, which almost nobody does, is the post-mortem: looking back, after the removal is complete, at how the whole process went, what went well, what went badly, and what you would do differently next time. Deprecations are skills that organisations get better at with practice, but only if they actively reflect on each one. The organisations I have seen do this well treat deprecations as first-class engineering work, with owners and timelines and explicit success criteria. The organisations that do not treat them this way end up doing every deprecation badly, learning nothing, and accumulating an ever-growing backlog of "things we wish we could deprecate but cannot because we never got around to setting up the process."

There is, finally, a broader point I want to make, which is that deprecation is fundamentally about respect. When you deprecate a feature, you are asking your users to do work — to update their integrations, to learn new patterns, to test their systems against your changes — and that work has a cost for them. Doing the deprecation well is the way you compensate them for that cost: by giving them clear information, by giving them adequate time, by giving them good tools, by treating their constraints as constraints you take seriously. Doing it badly is, in effect, treating their time as worthless. The reputation you build for handling deprecations well or badly will, over years, become one of the most important things people know about your project. It is worth investing in.
