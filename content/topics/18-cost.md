---
title: Cost and cloud spend
slug: cost
---

I want to talk about cost — specifically, about the cost of running the system in production, and how that cost evolves as the system grows. This is a topic that gets very little attention in most engineering discussions and that, in my experience, accounts for an outsized share of the reasons that promising projects ultimately fail. A system that is technically excellent but economically unviable is, in the long run, indistinguishable from a system that is technically broken. Both of them end in the same place.

The first thing I want to say is that cost is a technical concern, not just a business concern. The cost of running the system is determined by the architecture of the system, by the algorithms it uses, by the data structures it stores, by the network calls it makes, by the database queries it issues. Every one of those choices has a price tag attached to it, denominated in dollars per month, and at scale those price tags compound in ways that are very hard to predict from looking at any single component in isolation. A query that costs a fraction of a cent at a hundred queries per second costs ten thousand dollars a month at a million queries per second, and that is before you have factored in the cost of the load it puts on the database, the cost of the bandwidth it consumes, the cost of the storage it requires for caching, and the cost of the on-call engineer who has to babysit it when things go wrong.

So I would urge you, as you build, to think about cost the way you think about latency: as a property of the system that needs to be measured, budgeted, monitored, and optimised. The systems with the healthiest cost structures are the systems whose engineers know, at any given time, what each request costs and where the money is going. The systems with the unhealthiest cost structures are the systems whose engineers find out, only when the bill arrives, that some innocuous-looking feature is responsible for half the cloud spend.

The second thing I want to say is that the cost model of modern cloud providers is far more complex than most engineers realise. Compute is priced one way; storage another; network egress yet another. Reads from a managed database are often free; writes are often not. Cross-zone traffic is sometimes free; cross-region traffic is rarely free; cross-cloud traffic is usually punishingly expensive. The marginal cost of a particular operation depends on which service is performing it, in which region, against which data, with which level of redundancy. It is entirely possible to design a system that is fast, reliable, and elegant, and that is also dramatically more expensive than the equivalent system designed by someone who happens to have read the pricing page more carefully. Every cloud provider has cost surprises lurking in their pricing model, and the only way to avoid being caught by them is to actually read the pricing page, model the costs explicitly, and monitor them in production.

The third thing is the question of egress. Cloud providers, almost universally, charge much more for data leaving their network than for data entering it. This pricing asymmetry has shaped the modern industry in ways that are often invisible until you trip over them. A system that serves large amounts of data — video, images, large API responses — can find that its egress bill dwarfs every other line item in its cloud invoice. The mitigations are complex and not always available: CDNs reduce egress at the cost of additional complexity and additional dependencies; client-side caching reduces egress at the cost of data freshness; private network peering reduces egress at the cost of significant engineering investment. There is no free lunch here, and any system that ignores egress costs early on will eventually be forced to confront them, usually under pressure.

The fourth thing is the question of over-provisioning. Most systems are provisioned for peak load, with the result that they are running at a fraction of their capacity most of the time. This is, in some sense, the right answer — running out of capacity at peak is much worse than paying for unused capacity off-peak — but the ratio matters enormously. A system that needs ten times its average capacity at peak is dramatically more expensive than a system that needs three times its average. Reducing this ratio, through caching, queueing, batching, auto-scaling, or graceful degradation, is one of the highest-leverage cost optimisations available. It is also one of the most consistently neglected, because the benefits accrue slowly and the engineering work is unglamorous.

The fifth thing, which I will end on, is the question of dependencies. Every third-party service you integrate with has a price, and that price will, in almost all cases, scale with usage in ways that are difficult to predict in advance. A monitoring vendor that charges per host is cheap at one host and expensive at one thousand. An analytics platform that charges per event is cheap at low traffic and expensive at high traffic. An LLM API that charges per token is cheap for a prototype and ruinous for a production application that gets popular faster than expected. Every dependency you add is a future cost, and the calculation of "is this dependency worth it?" needs to include not just today's cost but the trajectory of tomorrow's. The systems that have stayed solvent at scale are the ones whose engineers asked, before adding each dependency, "what does this cost at ten times my current usage, and at a hundred times, and at a thousand times, and is that cost still acceptable?"

I will stop here, but I want to make one final observation. The conversations about cost in most engineering organisations are conducted, almost exclusively, by finance people, after the fact, when the bill has arrived. They should be conducted by engineers, in advance, when the architecture is being chosen. The decisions are technical; the consequences are financial; the responsibility is shared.
