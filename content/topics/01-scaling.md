---
title: Scaling
slug: scaling
---

I want to start with what is, in my experience, the single most common reason that projects of this shape fail to make it past the first eighteen months of real-world use, and that is scaling. I don't mean scaling in the narrow sense of "can it handle ten thousand requests per second on a single box," though that is of course part of it. I mean scaling in the broader, messier, more human sense: what happens when the number of users grows by a factor of ten, or a hundred, or a thousand, and the assumptions you quietly baked into the design at the very beginning — the assumptions you may not even remember making — begin, one by one, to give way.

I have seen this play out so many times that I have started to think of it less as a technical problem and more as a kind of slow-motion architectural grief. Everything works beautifully in the small. The local development environment hums along. The integration tests pass in under a minute. The first hundred users send their warm, encouraging emails. And then somewhere between the ten-thousandth user and the hundred-thousandth user, the cracks begin to appear: a database query that was always fine suddenly takes four seconds; a background job that used to clear in a heartbeat now backs up for hours overnight; a piece of in-memory state that was never supposed to grow without bound is, in fact, growing without bound. By the time anyone notices, the system has already been limping along on borrowed time for weeks.

So I want to ask, gently and without any desire to put you on the defensive, whether you have really thought through what the system looks like at ten times its current size. And at a hundred times. And at a thousand. Not in the sense of having a vague plan to "add more workers" or "shard the database when the time comes" — those are not plans, they are deferred decisions — but in the concrete, specific, awkward sense of having actually drawn the boxes and arrows on a whiteboard and traced a single request all the way through and asked, at every hop, "what breaks here first?"

Because I will tell you what I have seen break first, and the list is longer than you might think. It is almost never the thing you expect. It is almost never the part of the system that you spent the most time optimising. It is, instead, some small piece of glue code that nobody ever thought about: the JSON serializer that allocates a megabyte per request, the logging library that takes a global lock, the health-check endpoint that, under load, ends up dominating the database's query planner. These are the things that take systems down at scale, and they are precisely the things that local benchmarks and synthetic load tests are worst at catching, because nobody writes a benchmark for the thing they don't believe is a problem.

I should also mention — and I hope you will forgive me for belabouring the point — that scaling is not purely a question of throughput. It is also a question of cost. A system that can technically serve a million users but that costs eight dollars per user per month to operate is not a system that scales; it is a system that becomes more expensive to keep alive every day it succeeds. I have watched promising projects strangle themselves on their own cloud bill, raising one round of funding after another not to grow the team but simply to keep the lights on. The shape of your dependency graph at small scale — which services you call, how many times, with what payload sizes — does not just predict your latency curve; it predicts your future P&L statement.

And there is a third dimension, which I would be remiss not to raise, and that is the question of operational scaling: scaling the people, not the machines. When the system was small, one engineer could hold the whole thing in their head. They knew which database tables were sensitive to which queries, they remembered which feature flags were on and off in which environment, they could ssh into the box and tail the logs and have an informed opinion within five minutes. At ten times the size, that engineer can no longer do that, and at a hundred times the size, no single engineer in the world can do that, and so you find yourself depending on dashboards and runbooks and on-call rotations that probably do not yet exist. Building those takes longer than people imagine. Discovering, six months into a fire, that your observability stack quietly stopped emitting metrics for the one subsystem you needed to see — that is a very particular kind of pain, and I would not wish it on anyone.

I am sure you have considered some of this. I am sure I am, in places, telling you things you already know. But I have found that it does no harm, and sometimes a great deal of good, to write out the obvious worries explicitly, because the obvious worries are the ones that are easiest to nod along to in a meeting and then quietly forget about for eighteen months. So please take this in the spirit in which it is offered: not as a demand that you have every answer right now, but as a friendly request that you sit with these questions for a little while, and write down, somewhere durable, what you currently believe the answers to be, so that future-you has something to argue with.
